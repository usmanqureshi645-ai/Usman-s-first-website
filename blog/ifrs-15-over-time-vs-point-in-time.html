Whether revenue is recognised over time or at a point in time is not a policy choice under IFRS 15 — it is a determination driven by when the customer obtains control of the promised good or service (IFRS 15.31). Applying the wrong pattern misstates the shape of the income statement across the whole life of a contract, which is exactly why it draws intense auditor scrutiny. This guide works through the three over-time criteria in IFRS 15.35, the point-in-time control indicators in IFRS 15.38, the measurement-of-progress requirements in IFRS 15.39–45, and how they play out in construction, software and real estate, with worked journals, ISA-anchored red flags and a real filed case study.

When Does IFRS 15 Require Over-Time Recognition?

An entity recognises revenue over time if any one of the three criteria in IFRS 15.35 is met; if none is met, control transfers at a point in time and revenue is recognised then (IFRS 15.32, 15.38). The three tests are assessed at contract inception, before any progress measurement, and only one needs to be satisfied.

The first criterion, IFRS 15.35(a), is met where the customer simultaneously receives and consumes the benefits of the entity's performance as the entity performs. Recurring service contracts — cleaning, transaction processing, most software-as-a-service subscriptions — are the classic case: if another entity would not need to substantially re-perform the work done to date to take over, the benefits are being consumed as delivered (IFRS 15.B3–B4). The second criterion, IFRS 15.35(b), is met where the entity's performance creates or enhances an asset the customer controls as it is created or enhanced — think a building constructed on the customer's own land, or work-in-progress the customer directs and can stop (IFRS 15.B5).

The third criterion, IFRS 15.35(c), is the one that generates the most disputes. It is met only where the entity's performance does not create an asset with an alternative use to the entity (IFRS 15.36, expanded in 15.B6–B8) and the entity has an enforceable right to payment for performance completed to date (IFRS 15.37, 15.B9–B13). Both limbs are mandatory. "No alternative use" means contractual or practical restrictions prevent the entity from readily redirecting the asset to another customer — heavy customisation is the usual driver. The right to payment must, throughout the contract, entitle the entity to an amount that at least compensates it for costs incurred plus a reasonable margin (not just costs, and not merely a damages claim) and must be legally enforceable in the relevant jurisdiction (IFRS 15.B11–B12). Fail either limb and the contract defaults to point-in-time recognition on delivery.

How Do You Know Control Has Transferred at a Point in Time?

Where none of the IFRS 15.35 criteria is met, the entity recognises revenue at the point in time the customer obtains control of the asset (IFRS 15.38), and control means the ability to direct the use of, and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits from, the asset (IFRS 15.31–33). Control includes the ability to prevent others from directing the use of or obtaining the benefits (IFRS 15.33).

IFRS 15.38 lists five indicators that control has transferred, none individually decisive — they are weighed together: (a) the entity has a present right to payment for the asset; (b) the customer has legal title; (c) the entity has transferred physical possession; (d) the customer has the significant risks and rewards of ownership; and (e) the customer has accepted the asset. Physical possession and legal title can be misleading in isolation — a bill-and-hold arrangement (IFRS 15.B79–B82) or a repurchase agreement (IFRS 15.B64–B76) can leave control with the seller despite title passing, or transfer control despite goods remaining on the seller's site. The indicators are a control test, not a risks-and-rewards test inherited from IAS 18; risks and rewards is only one of five signals now.

How Do You Measure Progress Over Time?

Once over-time recognition is established, the entity selects a single method that best depicts the transfer of control and applies it consistently to similar contracts (IFRS 15.39–40). The methods fall into two families — output and input — and revenue is only recognised to the extent progress can be reasonably measured (IFRS 15.44).

Output methods (IFRS 15.41, B15) measure results transferred to date relative to total promised — surveys of performance completed, milestones reached, units delivered, or appraisals of results achieved. They most faithfully depict transfer but require reliable measurement of outputs, which is often not available part-way through a bespoke build. As a practical expedient, if an entity has a right to invoice an amount that corresponds directly to the value delivered to date (e.g. a fixed rate per hour), it may recognise revenue at that invoiced amount (IFRS 15.B16).

Input methods (IFRS 15.41, B18) measure the entity's efforts or inputs — costs incurred, labour hours, machine hours, time elapsed — relative to total expected inputs. The cost-to-cost input method dominates construction. Its key weaknesses are addressed directly in the standard: where a cost incurred is not proportionate to progress (for example uninstalled materials, or significant inefficiencies and wasted materials), the measure must be adjusted — IFRS 15.B19 requires uninstalled goods to be recognised at cost with zero margin where control has passed but the good is not yet installed, and IFRS 15.B19(b) excludes abnormal wastage from the progress calculation entirely. Where progress cannot be reasonably measured but the entity expects to recover its costs, revenue is recognised only to the extent of costs incurred until it can (IFRS 15.45) — a zero-margin "recoverable cost" approach.

How Do the Rules Apply to Construction, SaaS and Real Estate?

The same IFRS 15.35 tests give different answers across sectors because the underlying facts differ. In construction, contracts typically qualify over time under 15.35(b) — the customer controls the asset as it is built on their land — or under 15.35(c) where a bespoke asset has no alternative use and the contract carries an enforceable right to payment for work to date; cost-to-cost (IFRS 15.B18) is the near-universal measure, replacing the old IAS 11 stage-of-completion model.

In SaaS, a hosted subscription the customer accesses continuously usually meets 15.35(a) — the customer consumes the benefit daily — and is recognised straight-line over the term unless usage is uneven. But careful unbundling is needed: material upfront implementation, a software licence that conveys a right to use rather than access, or a distinct term licence (IFRS 15.B58–B62) may each be separate performance obligations with their own timing. In real estate, the answer hinges on the sale-and-purchase agreement: an off-plan residential unit sold with staged, non-refundable payments and a right to payment plus margin on cancellation can qualify over time under 15.35(c), whereas a completed unit or one the developer can readily resell transfers at completion — a point that produced significant divergence in practice and remains a hotspot for auditor challenge.

Worked Example: Input-Method Journals

A contractor signs a fixed-price contract for £5,000,000. Estimated total costs are £4,000,000 over 24 months. The asset is built on the customer's land, so over-time recognition applies under IFRS 15.35(b); the entity uses the cost-to-cost input method (IFRS 15.B18). In year 1, £1,200,000 of costs are incurred, all proportionate to progress. Progress = £1,200,000 / £4,000,000 = 30%. Revenue for year 1 = 30% × £5,000,000 = £1,500,000; cost of sales = £1,200,000; gross profit = £300,000. Assume £1,000,000 has been billed and £900,000 received.

The net £500,000 contract asset (£1,500,000 revenue recognised less £1,000,000 billed) is a right to consideration conditional on continued performance, disclosed under IFRS 15.105–107. If billings had exceeded revenue, the surplus would instead be a contract liability. Now vary the facts: suppose £300,000 of the £1,200,000 spend was for steel delivered to site but not yet installed, and the customer controls it. Under IFRS 15.B19, that £300,000 is recognised as revenue at cost (zero margin) and excluded from the progress percentage. Adjusted progress = (£1,200,000 − £300,000) / (£4,000,000 − £300,000) = 24.3%, giving £1,138,378 of margin-bearing revenue plus £300,000 at cost — a materially different result that a naive cost-to-cost calculation would overstate.

Auditor Red Flags and ISA References

Over-time revenue is an estimate-heavy, judgement-heavy area, so it maps directly onto the auditor's estimate and risk standards. Three recurring red flags:

  • Over-time asserted without an enforceable right to payment. Management applies 15.35(c) on a bespoke build but cannot produce legal evidence that, on cancellation, it recovers cost plus margin (IFRS 15.B12). Under ISA 540 (Revised) the auditor must challenge the method-selection assumption and obtain the contract and, where needed, a legal view; an unsupported enforceable-right conclusion is a control-transfer misstatement risk.
  • Cost-to-cost manipulated through estimated total costs. Because revenue = (costs to date / estimated total costs) × price, lowering the total-cost denominator accelerates revenue. ISA 240 treats revenue recognition as a presumed fraud risk; the auditor should retrospectively review prior-period out-turn estimates against actuals and test for optimistic re-forecasting near period end.
  • Uninstalled materials and abnormal wastage left in the progress base. Failing to strip out IFRS 15.B19 items inflates the percentage complete. Under ISA 330, substantive testing of cost cut-off and a reconciliation of costs incurred to physical progress (site surveys, QS reports) provide the corroborating evidence; analytical-only procedures are insufficient for a significant risk.
Red flag: Revenue recognised over time with no documented, contemporaneous assessment of which IFRS 15.35 criterion was met. Absent that inception-date memo, the auditor cannot conclude the pattern is supportable and will push toward point-in-time recognition until control transfer is evidenced (ISA 540, ISA 500).

Case Study: Balfour Beatty plc

Company. Balfour Beatty plc, the UK-listed infrastructure group, reported revenue of £10.0bn for the year ended 31 December 2024 (2023: £9.6bn), with a year-end order book of £18.4bn (2023: £16.5bn) and Construction Services profit from operations of £159m.

Policy. Balfour Beatty recognises revenue on long-term construction and support-services contracts over time, measuring progress using a cost-to-cost input method (IFRS 15.B18). Its financial statements flag the recognition of long-term contract revenue as a critical accounting judgement: management must forecast the final out-turn of each contract — total costs including maintenance and defects liabilities on the cost side, and variations, claims and potential liquidated damages on the income (variable consideration) side.

Why it matters. Because revenue tracks the ratio of costs incurred to forecast total costs, the whole income statement hinges on those forecasts — precisely the ISA 540 estimate and ISA 240 revenue-fraud risk described above. Balfour Beatty also discloses (Note 4.3, Annual Report 2024) the transaction price allocated to remaining performance obligations, the IFRS 15.120 backlog disclosure, and notes this statutory measure differs from its wider £18.4bn order book, which includes its share of joint-venture orders.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is over-time or point-in-time an accounting policy choice? No. It is a determination based on when the customer obtains control (IFRS 15.31–38). If any IFRS 15.35 criterion is met the entity must recognise over time; if none is met it must recognise at a point in time.

Does "no alternative use" on its own trigger over-time recognition? No. Under IFRS 15.35(c) the no-alternative-use condition must be paired with an enforceable right to payment for performance to date including a reasonable margin (IFRS 15.37, B9–B13). Only one limb, and the criterion fails.

What is the difference between input and output methods? Output methods (IFRS 15.B15) measure results delivered — units, milestones, surveys; input methods (IFRS 15.B18) measure effort consumed — costs, hours. Cost-to-cost is the most common input method in construction.

How are uninstalled materials treated under cost-to-cost? IFRS 15.B19 requires them to be recognised as revenue at cost with zero margin, and excluded from the progress percentage, so that pre-delivered materials do not overstate stage of completion.

What happens if progress cannot be reasonably measured? Under IFRS 15.44–45, if the entity expects to recover its costs it recognises revenue only to the extent of costs incurred (zero margin) until it can reasonably measure progress.

Did IFRS 15 replace IAS 11 for construction contracts? Yes. IFRS 15 superseded IAS 11 and IAS 18. The percentage-of-completion concept survives in substance as the cost-to-cost input method, but it is now framed around transfer of control rather than a stand-alone long-term-contract model.

Can a SaaS contract be recognised at a point in time? The hosted subscription itself usually meets IFRS 15.35(a) and is recognised over time, but a distinct on-premise software licence granting a right to use may transfer at a point in time (IFRS 15.B58–B61), so multi-element SaaS deals often mix both patterns.
