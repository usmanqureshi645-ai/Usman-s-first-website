Contract assets (unbilled receivables) and contract liabilities (deferred revenue) are the balance sheet consequences of the timing mismatch between when an entity satisfies a performance obligation and when the customer pays. Both arise directly from step 5 of the IFRS 15 model, but they sit at opposite ends of it: a contract asset means the entity has run ahead of billing, while a contract liability means the customer has paid ahead of delivery. Getting the distinction right matters because a contract asset is not a trade receivable, and mislabelling one as the other flatters the quality of the debtor book, distorts working-capital metrics, and carries different expected-credit-loss consequences. This guide sets out the recognition rules, the presentation and disclosure requirements, the IFRS 9 impairment interaction, worked journal entries, the auditor's testing focus, and a real-company case study drawn from Vodafone's IFRS financial statements.

What is a contract asset under IFRS 15?

A contract asset is an entity's right to consideration in exchange for goods or services it has already transferred to a customer, where that right is conditional on something other than the passage of time (IFRS 15.107 and Appendix A). In plain terms, the entity has earned the revenue and recognised it, but it cannot yet bill the customer because payment still depends on completing further performance or meeting some other milestone. IFRS 15.105 requires an entity, when either party has performed, to present the contract in the statement of financial position as a contract asset or a contract liability depending on the relationship between the entity's performance and the customer's payment.

The defining feature is conditionality. Under IFRS 15.107, if an entity performs by transferring goods or services before the customer pays or before payment is due, the entity presents the contract as a contract asset, excluding any amounts presented as a receivable. Common examples include long-term construction and engineering contracts recognised over time under IFRS 15.35, professional-services engagements billed on completion of a phase, and bundled arrangements where an upfront-delivered element (say, hardware) is only invoiced as later service periods elapse. Contract assets are frequently described as "unbilled receivables", "accrued income", or "amounts recoverable on contracts" in the financial statements, but IFRS 15.109 permits alternative descriptions provided sufficient information is given for users to distinguish them from receivables.

What is a contract liability (deferred revenue) under IFRS 15?

A contract liability is an entity's obligation to transfer goods or services to a customer for which the entity has already received consideration, or for which consideration is due, from that customer (IFRS 15.106 and Appendix A). It is the mirror image of a contract asset: the customer has run ahead of the entity, so the cash collected cannot yet be recognised as revenue and is instead parked on the balance sheet until the related performance obligation is satisfied. IFRS 15.106 requires that if a customer pays consideration, or the entity has an unconditional right to an amount of consideration (a receivable), before the entity transfers the good or service, the entity presents the contract as a contract liability when the payment is made or due, whichever is earlier.

Deferred revenue, unearned revenue, subscriptions in advance, and upfront deposits are all everyday forms of contract liability. A twelve-month software subscription billed and collected on day one, an annual maintenance plan, an airline ticket sold before the flight, or a gym membership paid yearly all create a contract liability that is released to revenue as the performance obligation is satisfied over time under IFRS 15.35 or at the point in time control transfers under IFRS 15.38. The balance is not a financing arrangement unless the timing difference is significant (IFRS 15.60), in which case the entity may need to adjust for a significant financing component and accrete interest on the liability.

How does a contract asset differ from a trade receivable?

A contract asset carries a conditional right to consideration, whereas a trade receivable carries an unconditional right where only the passage of time is required before payment is due (IFRS 15.108). This is the single most important classification distinction in the standard, because the two line items are recognised, presented, and disclosed separately and are read very differently by users. IFRS 15.105 explicitly distinguishes a receivable from a contract asset, and IFRS 15.108 states that a receivable is recognised when the entity's right to consideration is unconditional, meaning nothing but time stands between the entity and the cash.

The practical test is: what has to happen before the entity can demand payment? If only time must pass, for example an invoice has been raised on 30-day terms, it is a receivable. If further performance, customer acceptance, achievement of a milestone, or any other condition remains outstanding, it is a contract asset. The moment that condition is met, typically when the entity gains the unconditional right to bill, the contract asset is reclassified to a trade receivable with the entry Dr Trade receivables / Cr Contract asset; no revenue is recognised on that reclassification because the revenue was already booked when the performance obligation was satisfied. Presenting a conditional contract asset within trade receivables overstates the apparent liquidity and collectability of the debtor book and can mislead users assessing days-sales-outstanding.

How are contract assets and liabilities presented and disclosed?

Contract assets and contract liabilities are presented on a net basis at the level of the individual contract, and separately disclosed from receivables, with a suite of qualitative and quantitative disclosures required by IFRS 15.116 to 118. Within a single contract, the rights and obligations are combined and presented as a single net contract asset or net contract liability position (IFRS 15.105); the netting does not extend across different contracts. An entity uses the alternative descriptions it chooses, but must give enough information for users to distinguish contract assets and contract liabilities from receivables (IFRS 15.109).

IFRS 15.116 requires disclosure of the opening and closing balances of receivables, contract assets, and contract liabilities. IFRS 15.116(b) requires disclosure of revenue recognised in the reporting period that was included in the contract liability balance at the beginning of the period, the classic "how much of last year's deferred revenue did we earn" figure. IFRS 15.117 requires an explanation of how the timing of satisfaction of performance obligations relates to the typical timing of payment and the effect on the contract asset and contract liability balances. IFRS 15.118 requires an explanation of the significant changes in the balances during the period, including qualitative and quantitative movements such as business combinations, cumulative catch-up adjustments, impairment of contract assets, and changes in the time frame for a right to consideration becoming unconditional. Separately, IFRS 15.120 to 122 require disclosure of the aggregate transaction price allocated to remaining (unsatisfied) performance obligations, which is a broader forward-looking measure than the contract liability balance alone.

Worked examples: journal entries

Example 1 — Contract liability (SaaS subscription). A vendor sells a 12-month SaaS subscription for £12,000, invoiced and collected in full on 1 January. The service is delivered evenly over the year, so the performance obligation is satisfied over time under IFRS 15.35(a). Revenue is released monthly and the contract liability unwinds to nil by year end.

Example 2 — Contract asset then reclassification. A consultancy agrees a £100,000 fixed-price project with two milestones. It may only invoice £60,000 once milestone 1 (client acceptance) is signed off, and the remaining £40,000 on final delivery. By 31 March it has satisfied 50% of the single performance obligation measured on an input basis (IFRS 15.41) but milestone 1 has not yet been accepted, so it has no unconditional right to bill. Revenue of £50,000 is recognised against a contract asset because the right to payment is still conditional on client acceptance (IFRS 15.107).

DateTransactionJournal entry
31 Mar50% performance satisfied, not yet billableDr Contract asset £50,000 | Cr Revenue £50,000
15 AprMilestone 1 accepted; £60,000 now invoiceableDr Trade receivables £60,000 | Cr Contract asset £50,000 | Cr Contract liability £10,000
—(£10,000 billed ahead of performance sits as a contract liability until earned)
30 JunRemaining performance satisfiedDr Contract liability £10,000 | Cr Revenue £10,000

Note in Example 2 that the reclassification on 15 April recognises no additional revenue; it simply converts a conditional right into an unconditional one and books the small over-billing as a contract liability. This split-position outcome, part receivable and part contract liability on the same contract, is exactly why IFRS 15.105 requires netting at the contract level rather than presenting each leg gross.

Impairment of contract assets under IFRS 9

Contract assets are within the scope of the IFRS 9 impairment model, and an impairment of a contract asset is measured, presented, and disclosed on the same basis as a financial asset within the scope of IFRS 9 (IFRS 15.107 and IFRS 15.113(b)). In practice this means recognising expected credit losses (ECLs) on contract assets from initial recognition, not waiting for objective evidence of default. Because a contract asset is not itself a financial asset, IFRS 9.5.5.15 permits entities to apply the simplified approach and measure the loss allowance at an amount equal to lifetime expected credit losses, using a provision matrix in the same way as for trade receivables.

Any impairment loss on a contract asset is presented in profit or loss, and IFRS 15.113(b) requires it to be disclosed separately from impairment losses on other contracts. A common error is to apply ECL only to invoiced receivables and forget the unbilled contract-asset balance, which can be material for long-cycle construction and engineering entities. The credit-risk assessment should reflect the specific counterparty, the sector, forward-looking macroeconomic information, and any deterioration since the revenue was first recognised. Where the contract asset relates to a customer whose credit has deteriorated, the entity must increase the loss allowance even though no invoice has yet been raised.

Watch the ordering. Impairment of a contract asset (an IFRS 9 credit question) is distinct from whether the contract is onerous (an IAS 37 question) and from whether the transaction price is constrained (an IFRS 15.56 variable-consideration question). Test them separately: a fully collectable contract asset can still sit on a loss-making contract, and a profitable contract can still carry ECL if the counterparty is weak.

Auditor red flags & ISA procedures

For an auditor, contract asset and contract liability balances are prime territory for both error and manipulation, because they sit at the intersection of revenue timing and management judgement. The following red flags each map to a specific ISA procedure.

  • Conditional balances parked in trade receivables. If unbilled amounts are classified as receivables, days-sales-outstanding looks artificially healthy and the debtor ageing is misstated. Under ISA 500, obtain the contract, trace the "receivable" back to an actual invoice or unconditional billing right, and inspect the milestone/acceptance terms; a balance with no invoice and an outstanding performance condition is a contract asset, not a receivable (IFRS 15.108). Test a sample of the largest and oldest unbilled balances.
  • Deferred revenue released too early to hit targets. Premature release of contract liabilities is a classic revenue-recognition fraud risk. Under ISA 240, which presumes a fraud risk in revenue recognition, and ISA 330's responsive substantive procedures, perform cut-off testing around period end, recompute the deferred-revenue waterfall, and reconcile the IFRS 15.116(b) disclosure (revenue recognised from the opening contract liability) to the movement schedule. Unexplained acceleration in the release rate is a flag.
  • Contract assets not impaired despite deteriorating counterparties. Management often applies ECL to invoiced debtors only. Under ISA 540 (auditing accounting estimates), evaluate the ECL model's coverage of the unbilled contract-asset balance, challenge the provision-matrix loss rates against forward-looking data, and test whether known credit deterioration on specific customers has been reflected (IFRS 15.107, IFRS 9 simplified approach).

Real-company case study: Vodafone Group Plc

Filing. Vodafone Group Plc, Annual Report for the year ended 31 March 2024 (prepared under UK-adopted / IFRS Accounting Standards), revenue disclosure note.

Vodafone is a useful illustration because its telecoms business generates large contract liabilities from customers who pay in advance (handset plans, prepaid airtime, annual and monthly service bundles). The Group disclosed total contract liabilities of €1,908 million at 31 March 2024, split between current and non-current:

Consistent with the IFRS 15.116(b) disclosure requirement, Vodafone explained that materially all of the €2,043 million recorded as current contract liabilities at 1 April 2023 was recognised as revenue during the year (with a portion reclassified to discontinued operations on the disposal of its Italy and Spain businesses). This is exactly the "how much of last year's deferred revenue did we earn" figure that step-5 disclosures are designed to surface, and it demonstrates the characteristic short cycle of a subscription-driven contract liability: the opening current balance is almost entirely released within twelve months.

Takeaway. The current-versus-non-current split (€1,565m against €343m) tells users that the vast majority of Vodafone's advance-collected consideration will convert to revenue within a year, while a smaller tail relates to longer-dated obligations. Analysts read a falling contract-liability balance carefully: it can signal genuine timing shifts (as here, partly driven by disposals) rather than a fall in underlying demand, which is precisely why IFRS 15.118 requires an explanation of significant changes in the balances.

Frequently asked questions

Is a contract asset the same as an unbilled receivable or accrued income? In substance, yes. "Unbilled receivable", "accrued income", and "amounts recoverable on contracts" are common labels for what IFRS 15 calls a contract asset. IFRS 15.109 permits alternative descriptions provided the entity gives enough information to distinguish the balance from an ordinary trade receivable.

Is deferred revenue a liability or income? Deferred revenue is a liability, not income. It is a contract liability under IFRS 15.106 representing an obligation to deliver goods or services for which the customer has already paid. It only becomes revenue as the related performance obligation is satisfied.

Do contract assets get impaired? Yes. Contract assets are subject to the IFRS 9 expected-credit-loss model (IFRS 15.107). Entities typically use the IFRS 9 simplified approach and recognise lifetime ECL from initial recognition, on the same basis as trade receivables.

When does a contract asset become a trade receivable? When the entity's right to consideration becomes unconditional, meaning only the passage of time remains before payment is due (IFRS 15.108). At that point the balance is reclassified Dr Trade receivables / Cr Contract asset, with no additional revenue recognised.

Can a single contract have both a contract asset and a contract liability? Not simultaneously as separate line items. IFRS 15.105 requires the rights and obligations within one contract to be netted into a single contract asset or contract liability position. Across different contracts, however, an entity will report contract assets on some and contract liabilities on others.

What is the difference between a contract liability and remaining performance obligations? A contract liability (IFRS 15.106) reflects consideration already received or due for undelivered goods or services. The remaining performance obligations disclosure (IFRS 15.120) is broader: it shows the total transaction price allocated to all unsatisfied obligations, including amounts not yet invoiced or collected.

Where do contract assets and liabilities sit on the balance sheet? Contract assets are presented separately from trade receivables, and both are split between current and non-current based on the expected timing of settlement. IFRS 15.105 to 109 govern presentation, and IFRS 15.116 to 118 the movement and change disclosures.
